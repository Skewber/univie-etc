Methods & formalism

How the exposure time calculator turns inputs into numbers. All formulas point to the function that implements them in app.js or sources.js.

Overview

The calculator evaluates the standard CCD signal-to-noise equation for a target observed through one of the broadband filters. The full chain is:

  1. Convert the entered magnitude to a detected electron rate $R_\star$ using the per-filter zero point and extinction.
  2. Build the per-pixel sky rate $R_\mathrm{sky}$ (including the moon contribution) and dark rate $R_\mathrm{dark}$.
  3. Plug into the SNR equation. Depending on the solve mode, either evaluate SNR for a given time or invert the equation for time given a target SNR.
  4. Check peak-pixel saturation, compute the limiting magnitude, and break down the noise variance.

The math has not changed since the calculator was first deployed. Only the numbers behind the symbols change: the instrumental constants below are loaded live from constants.json so this page always shows what the calculator is actually using.

1. Photometry — magnitude to electron rate

For each broadband filter $f$ the pipeline measures a zero point $\mathrm{ZP}_f$ defined as the in-band magnitude that produces $1\ \mathrm{e^-/s}$ above the atmosphere, and a first-order extinction coefficient $k_f$ in mag/airmass. An object of magnitude $m$ observed at airmass $X$ produces a detected rate (fluxFor):

$$ R_\star \;=\; 10^{\,0.4\,\bigl(\mathrm{ZP}_f \;-\; (m + k_f\,X)\bigr)} \qquad [\mathrm{e^-/s}] $$

The inverse (magFor) turns a measured rate back into an in-band magnitude — used for the limiting magnitude and for the extended-source mean / peak surface brightness reported in the results table:

$$ m \;=\; \mathrm{ZP}_f - k_f\,X - 2.5\,\log_{10}(R_\star) $$

For extended sources $R_\star$ is the rate per arcsec² when the brightness is given as a surface brightness, otherwise it is the total source rate and the spatial distribution is set by the chosen profile (Section 6).

2. Noise model and the SNR equation

The detector noise has four independent contributions, all in electrons:

  • Source shot noise — Poisson variance equal to the integrated source signal $S\,t$.
  • Sky shot noise — Poisson variance per pixel equal to $R_\mathrm{sky}\,t$. Multiplied by the aperture pixel count $n_\mathrm{pix}$.
  • Dark current — Poisson variance per pixel $R_\mathrm{dark}\,t$, also multiplied by $n_\mathrm{pix}$.
  • Read noise — added once per readout, not per second. With $N_\mathrm{sub}$ sub-exposures the total read variance per pixel is $N_\mathrm{sub}\,N_R^2$.

Combining them, with $S$ the source rate in the extraction aperture and $n_\mathrm{pix}$ the aperture area in pixels (snrForTime):

$$ \mathrm{SNR} \;=\; \frac{S\,t}{\sqrt{\,S\,t \;+\; n_\mathrm{pix} \bigl[\,t\,(R_\mathrm{sky} + R_\mathrm{dark}) \;+\; N_\mathrm{sub}\,N_R^{\,2}\bigr]\,}} $$

The four variance contributions are what the doughnut chart in the main app shows as percentages. Whichever dominates tells you which dial to turn: source-limited → already photon-noise-bound; sky-limited → no benefit from longer subs; read-limited → use longer subs, slower readout (1 MHz), or binning; dark-limited → cool deeper.

3. Noise breakdown

The doughnut chart in the main app slices the total noise variance into its four contributions. Each is one term under the radical of the SNR equation:

$$ \begin{aligned} \sigma^2_\mathrm{source} &= S\,t \\ \sigma^2_\mathrm{sky} &= n_\mathrm{pix}\,R_\mathrm{sky}\,t \\ \sigma^2_\mathrm{dark} &= n_\mathrm{pix}\,R_\mathrm{dark}\,t \\ \sigma^2_\mathrm{read} &= n_\mathrm{pix}\,N_\mathrm{sub}\,N_R^{\,2} \end{aligned} $$

Three of the terms grow linearly with the integration time $t$; the read term does not — read noise is paid once per detector readout, so it scales with $N_\mathrm{sub}$ instead. The chart shows each as a percentage of the total:

$$ \sigma^2_\mathrm{tot} \;=\; \sigma^2_\mathrm{source} + \sigma^2_\mathrm{sky} + \sigma^2_\mathrm{dark} + \sigma^2_\mathrm{read} $$

The percentages are variance shares, not RMS shares. Noise terms add in quadrature, so the square roots would not sum to 100 %. The variance form is the one that tells you which dial is worth turning — halving a 50 % slice removes more total noise than halving a 5 % one, regardless of how the RMS values look side by side.

Reading the chart — four regimes

Dominant termWhat it meansWhat helps
Source (Poisson) Already at the photon-counting floor for this target Longer total integration; larger telescope
Sky Sky photons swamp the source in the aperture Smaller extraction aperture, narrower filter, lower airmass, no moon, darker site
Dark current Cooler set-point is too warm for this integration Lower the cooler set-point
Read noise Too many short subs — read noise is paid each one Longer subs, 1 MHz readout, on-chip binning

When is the read term negligible?

For a sub-exposure of length $L$, the read variance per unit integration time is $N_R^{\,2}/L$ per pixel. Comparing it to the sky variance rate $R_\mathrm{sky}$ per pixel:

$$ \frac{\sigma^2_\mathrm{read}}{\sigma^2_\mathrm{sky}} \;=\; \frac{N_R^{\,2}}{R_\mathrm{sky}\,L} $$

So the read term contributes less than ~10 % of the sky variance when

$$ L \;\gtrsim\; \frac{10\,N_R^{\,2}}{R_\mathrm{sky}} $$

Worked example — minimum sub length to be sky-limited. With $N_R^{8\mathrm{MHz}} = $  e−:

  • V band, dark sky ($R_\mathrm{sky} = $  e−/s/px): $L_\mathrm{min} \approx $  s.
  • I band, dark sky ($R_\mathrm{sky} = $  e−/s/px): $L_\mathrm{min} \approx $  s.

So in V under dark skies, subs of a couple of minutes already drop read noise to a ~10 % variance contribution; in I the sky is dimmer, so individual subs need to be longer or the breakdown stays read-noise-heavy. Bright sky (moon, twilight) shortens $L_\mathrm{min}$; narrowband filters lengthen it dramatically. That is the underlying reason narrowband observers run long single subs while broadband observers can split into short subs without paying much of a read-noise penalty.

4. Solving for exposure time

The "solve for time" mode inverts the SNR equation for $t$. The form of the solution depends on how $N_\mathrm{sub}$ scales with $t$.

Fixed number of reads

When $N_\mathrm{sub}$ is fixed independent of $t$ (single exposure, or a user-fixed sub count) the equation is a quadratic in $t$ (timeForSnrFixedReads). Squaring the SNR equation and rearranging:

$$ S^2\,t^2 \;-\; \mathrm{SNR}^2\bigl[S + n_\mathrm{pix}(R_\mathrm{sky}+R_\mathrm{dark})\bigr]\,t \;-\; \mathrm{SNR}^2\,n_\mathrm{pix}\,N_\mathrm{sub}\,N_R^{\,2} \;=\; 0 $$

Taking the positive root gives the time in closed form.

Fixed sub-exposure length

When each sub has a fixed length $L$, the read count grows linearly with total time: $N_\mathrm{sub} \approx t/L$. The read-noise term becomes a rate $n_\mathrm{pix} N_R^2 / L$ and the SNR equation is linear in $t$ (timeForSnrFixedSubLen):

$$ t \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{SNR}^2\,\bigl[\,S + n_\mathrm{pix}(R_\mathrm{sky}+R_\mathrm{dark}) + n_\mathrm{pix}\,N_R^{\,2}/L\,\bigr]}{S^{\,2}} $$

Worked example — quadratic solve. A V=15 point source at airmass $X = 1.2$, 2" seeing, 2×2 binning, 8 MHz readout, dark sky, single exposure, target SNR = 100.

From the constants in use: $\mathrm{ZP}_V = $ , $k_V = $ , $R_\mathrm{sky,V} = $ $\ \mathrm{e^-/s/px}$ (per bin-2 pixel — already what we want here), $N_R^{8\mathrm{MHz}} = $ $\ \mathrm{e^-}$, $R_\mathrm{dark}@{-5}^\circ$C $\approx$ $\ \mathrm{e^-/s/px}$.

Source rate: $R_\star = 10^{0.4(22.124 - (15 + 0.15\cdot 1.2))} \approx 781\ \mathrm{e^-/s}$. Aperture (3 × FWHM, pixel scale 0.559" at bin 2): radius $r = 1.5 \cdot 2/0.559 \approx 5.37$ px, so $n_\mathrm{pix} = \pi r^2 \approx 90.6$. Plugging into the quadratic and taking the positive root gives $t \approx 12.8$ s.

You can reproduce this in the calculator with the same inputs — the headline should agree.

5. Exposure structures

Four sub-exposure modes are available (resolveExposure):

  • Single — one frame of length $t$; read noise paid once.
  • Fixed sub-exposure length — sub length $L$ is given; $N_\mathrm{sub}$ is derived from the total time (rounded up to a whole sub). Time-solve uses the linear closed form above.
  • Fixed number of subs — $N_\mathrm{sub}$ is given; sub length is $t/N_\mathrm{sub}$. Time-solve uses the quadratic.
  • Fixed total time, auto-size subs — total $T$ is given; $N_\mathrm{sub}$ is chosen as the smallest integer such that each sub stays below the saturation safety threshold (default 70 % of full well). See the worked example below.

Worked example — sub count from saturation. The maximum sub length is found by requiring that the peak per-pixel signal stay below a fraction $\eta$ of the full well:

$$ t_\mathrm{sub,max} \;=\; \frac{\eta\,Q_\mathrm{sat}\,g}{R_\mathrm{peak} + R_\mathrm{sky} + R_\mathrm{dark}}, \qquad N_\mathrm{sub} \;=\; \lceil T / t_\mathrm{sub,max} \rceil $$

where $Q_\mathrm{sat}$ is the full-well in ADU () and $g$ is the gain ( $\mathrm{e^-/ADU}$). A bright star with $R_\mathrm{peak} = 5000\ \mathrm{e^-/s}$, dark sky ($R_\mathrm{sky} \approx 20\ \mathrm{e^-/s/px}$), $\eta = 0.7$ gives $t_\mathrm{sub,max} \approx \frac{0.7 \cdot 65535 \cdot 1.50}{5000 + 20} \approx 13.7$ s, so a $T = 600$ s total is split into $\lceil 600/13.7 \rceil = 44$ subs.

6. Saturation and peak pixel

The peak pixel accumulates source + sky + dark every second. For a point source the peak per-pixel rate is the central value of a Gaussian PSF (binned-pixel coordinates):

$$ R_\mathrm{peak,point} \;=\; \frac{R_\star}{2\pi\sigma^2}, \qquad \sigma = \frac{\mathrm{FWHM}}{2.3548\,s_\mathrm{bin}} $$

where $s_\mathrm{bin}$ is the binned-pixel scale in arcsec. For an extended source the peak SB is seeing-limited — the mean flux inside a seeing-FWHM disc centred on the source — so a cuspy profile does not saturate harder than the PSF allows.

The peak ADU in a frame of length $t_\mathrm{frame}$ (peakElectrons):

$$ \mathrm{peak\,ADU} \;=\; \bigl(R_\mathrm{peak} + R_\mathrm{sky} + R_\mathrm{dark}\bigr) \,t_\mathrm{frame}\,/\,g $$

Saturation is always checked per sub, never on the stacked total. The traffic-light box reports the result against the ADU full well.

7. Aperture and pixel area

For a point source the extraction aperture is a circle of diameter $D = a \cdot \mathrm{FWHM}$ (with the user's multiplier $a$, default $a = 3$) (nPixPoint):

$$ n_\mathrm{pix} \;=\; \pi \left(\frac{a\,\mathrm{FWHM}}{2\,s_\mathrm{bin}}\right)^{\!2} $$

For an extended source with a circular extraction aperture of radius $r_\mathrm{ap}$ (arcsec) and a profile with cumulative enclosed flux $f_\mathrm{enc}(r)$:

$$ S \;=\; F_\mathrm{tot}\,f_\mathrm{enc}(r_\mathrm{ap}), \qquad n_\mathrm{pix} \;=\; \pi\,r_\mathrm{ap}^2 / s_\mathrm{bin}^2 $$

where $F_\mathrm{tot}$ is the total source rate (either entered directly as a total magnitude, or derived from the entered mean surface brightness by inverting $F_\mathrm{tot} = \pi r_\mathrm{ap}^2 \cdot R_{\mathrm{SB}} / f_\mathrm{enc}(r_\mathrm{ap})$). The three profiles supported are (SRC.encFracBuilder):

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{uniform:}\ & f_\mathrm{enc}(r) = (r/R)^2 \ \text{for}\ r \le R,\ 1\ \text{else} \\ \text{Gaussian (FWHM = size):}\ & f_\mathrm{enc}(r) = 1 - \exp\!\bigl(-r^2/2\sigma^2\bigr),\ \sigma = \text{FWHM}/2.3548 \\ \text{S\'ersic } n\,(\mathrm{R_e} = \text{size}/2):\ & I(r) \propto \exp\!\bigl[-b_n\bigl((r/R_e)^{1/n} - 1\bigr)\bigr] \end{aligned} $$

The Sérsic cumulative is tabulated numerically out to $12 R_e$ at startup; lookup is linear interpolation.

8. Sky brightness — dark night plus moon

The dark-sky electron rate per pixel $R_\mathrm{sky,dark}$ was measured on a moonless night (column sky_rate_e_per_s_per_pix in constants.json). The total per-pixel sky rate used in the SNR equation is

$$ R_\mathrm{sky}(\mathrm{filter},b) \;=\; R_\mathrm{sky,dark}\, \cdot\,(b/2)^2 \,\cdot\, M_\mathrm{user} \,\cdot\, M_\mathrm{moon} $$

where $b$ is the binning (the measurement is at bin 2, $(b/2)^2$ scales it to other binnings), $M_\mathrm{user}$ is the manual "Sky brightness ×" multiplier, and $M_\mathrm{moon}$ is the Krisciunas & Schaefer (1991) moon-brightness model (moonFactor).

The moon model first turns the moon illumination fraction $f$ into the phase angle $\alpha$ (degrees from new moon), then the moon's out-of-atmosphere brightness $I^\star$, then the surface brightness it adds at angular separation $\rho$ (deg) and airmass $X$:

$$ \begin{aligned} \alpha &= \arccos(2f - 1) \\ I^\star &= 10^{-0.4(3.84 + 0.026|\alpha| + 4\!\times\!10^{-9}\alpha^4)} \\ f(\rho) &= 10^{5.36}(1.06 + \cos^2\rho) + 10^{6.15 - \rho/40} \\ B_\mathrm{moon} &= f(\rho)\,I^\star\,10^{-0.4\,k_V X}\bigl(1 - 10^{-0.4\,k_V X}\bigr) \end{aligned} $$

$B_\mathrm{moon}$ is in nanolamberts; converting to a V mag/arcsec² ($\mu_\mathrm{moon}$) and comparing to the measured dark-sky surface brightness $\mu_\mathrm{dark}$ gives the multiplicative factor:

$$ M_\mathrm{moon} \;=\; 1 + 10^{-0.4(\mu_\mathrm{moon} - \mu_\mathrm{dark})} $$

Worked example — half-moon at 60° from target. Illumination $f = 0.5$ gives $\alpha = 90°$ (a half moon, first or last quarter). $I^\star \approx 10^{-0.4 \cdot 6.50} \approx 2.5\times 10^{-3}$; $f(60°) \approx 10^{5.36}\cdot 1.31 + 10^{6.15-1.5} \approx 3.4\times 10^{5}$. At airmass 1.2 with $k_V = $ , the extinction factor $10^{-0.4 k_V X}(1 - 10^{-0.4 k_V X}) \approx 0.155$. Result: $B_\mathrm{moon} \approx 130$ nL, $\mu_\mathrm{moon} \approx 20.5$ mag/arcsec². The dark-sky V surface brightness comes from the measured per-pixel rate via the V zero point. The ratio gives $M_\mathrm{moon} \approx 4$ — i.e. the sky is roughly 4× brighter than on a moonless night. Enter $f = 0.5$ and separation = 60° in the calculator to see the live number.

Caveat: the model is V-band and is applied uniformly to all bands. It also assumes the moon is at the same altitude as the target; a low moon below the horizon does not raise the sky at zenith. Useful for planning, not for absolute photometry under bright moon.

9. Dark current versus temperature

The dark current is measured at one cooler set-point ( °C). For other temperatures it is scaled by the standard CCD doubling law (darkRate):

$$ R_\mathrm{dark}(T) \;=\; R_\mathrm{dark,ref}\,\cdot\, 2^{(T - T_\mathrm{ref}) / \Delta T_\mathrm{double}}\,\cdot\,(b/2)^2 $$

With $R_\mathrm{dark,ref} = $ $\mathrm{e^-/s/px}$ at $T_\mathrm{ref} = $  °C and a provisional doubling temperature $\Delta T_\mathrm{double} = $  K — the latter is a typical literature value and should be replaced with a fit to darks taken at several cooler set-points (see data_acquisition.md).

10. Limiting magnitude

The limiting magnitude reported in the results table is the faintest in-band point-source magnitude that reaches a fixed S/N ($\Sigma = 5$ by default) in the same aperture, total time, sub structure, and observing conditions. Solving the SNR equation as a quadratic in the source rate $S$:

$$ S^2\,t^2 \;-\; \Sigma^2\,S\,t \;-\; \Sigma^2\,B \;=\; 0, \qquad B \;=\; n_\mathrm{pix}\bigl[t(R_\mathrm{sky}+R_\mathrm{dark}) + N_\mathrm{sub}\,N_R^2\bigr] $$

and taking the positive root,

$$ S_\mathrm{lim} \;=\; \frac{\Sigma^2}{2t}\!\left(1 + \sqrt{1 + 4B/\Sigma^2}\right) $$

Converting back to a magnitude with the inverse zero-point relation gives $m_\mathrm{lim} = \mathrm{ZP}_f - k_f X - 2.5\log_{10} S_\mathrm{lim}$. The limit is reported only for point sources, because the equivalent for an extended source is a limiting surface brightness that depends on the chosen profile and aperture and is therefore less universal.

11. SED color helper (approximate)

If you know the magnitude in one band but want to observe in another, the calculator can apply an approximate color correction (SED.bandMag). Two options:

  • Blackbody: colors come from the Planck function at the chosen temperature, zero-pointed on Vega (so an A0V analogue has zero colors).
  • Spectral type / object template: a hard-coded table of $(B-V, V-R_c, V-I_c)$ values per type. Stellar values from Pecaut & Mamajek (2013); galaxy values from Fukugita+ 1995; emission-line object colors are approximate broadband averages.

V is used as the hub: the magnitude in any band is converted to V via the table, then to the observing band:

$$ m_V = m_\mathrm{ref} + \Delta m_{V \leftarrow \mathrm{ref}}, \qquad m_\mathrm{obs} = m_V + \Delta m_{\mathrm{obs} \leftarrow V} $$

Worked example — V mag of an M0 star observed in I. The M0 row in the table has $V-I_c = 1.62$. A star with $V = 14.0$ would appear in I-band at $m_I = m_V - (V-I_c) = 14.0 - 1.62 = 12.38$. The calculator reports both numbers in the results table when the SED is set to "M0".

Important caveat: the empirical zero point for each filter was anchored on stars (M67) whose SED is roughly solar. Applying it to a strongly-coloured source (cool M dwarfs, hot O stars, emission nebulae) introduces an error that the color helper does not correct for — it only handles the cross-band magnitude conversion. For emission-line objects in broadband filters the colors are very approximate. For an in-filter magnitude with no conversion, leave the SED on Flat (none).
